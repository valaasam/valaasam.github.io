Science in the Wild @Patagonia

Last week I was invited to participate in an event called “Science in the Wild”, hosted by our local Patagonia branch and organized by graduate students in the Department of Natural Resources from the University of Nevada. We each gave a 10-minute popular science talk about our research, emphasizing a “call to action” or specific actions the public can do to be a positive influence for local conservation. I was able to share my work about the effects of light pollution on birds, and some easy steps people can take to minimize impacts! Light pollution is an ecological concern that literally exists our backyards - it doesn’t get more local than that!

Here is my call to action:

Replace: Change any bright white light bulbs that are used outdoors to softer, more yellow hues. My research has shown that warmer colored lights are less disruptive for birds. Avoiding the short (blue) wavelengths that are present in whiter light is better for us humans trying to sleep too!

Redirect: Make sure the light we are using outdoors is going where it’s intended! That means getting covers for street or porch lamps so that they point downward at the street or sidewalk, and not out into the air or surrounding natural habitats. Additionally, use curtains to prevent the light use indoors from spilling out into the environment.

Reduce: On a broad scale, many cities have adopted “lights out” policies to reduce the amount of night light during critical life history events, such as migration or breeding. As individuals, we can reduce night light by investing in motion-sensors (so that lights only turn on when we need them!), or dimmers so that we can reduce the intensity of light when there is extra reflectance (like when there is snow cover).

Remove: Talk to your local policy makers or employers about removing lights that are redundant or unnecessary. It’s a win-win, reducing impacts on wildlife and saving everyone energy and money!

Advice and reflections from AOS Women in Science

At the 2019 American Ornithological Society Conference I was invited to be a part of a video series organized by the Conservation Media Program at the Cornell Lab of Ornithology, where we told stories about our scientific journey with the intention of showcasing diversity in science! For our 2019 meeting in Anchorage, Alaska, the American Ornithological Society worked with the Conservation Media Program at the Cornell Lab of Ornithology to produce three short videos of contemporary ornithologists telling the stories of themselves as scientists. Our objectives were to highlight diverse profiles of scientists, to show viewers that diversity is a welcome and indispensable part of a strong science community, and to contribute to the Diversity and Inclusion Committee’s work to reduce barriers to conference attendance.

Sometimes science-ing is hard. Spoiler alert - not getting enough sleep stresses birds out, and staying up all night to document it stresses me out too! Thankfully I have some amazing students and we get creative, finding ways to keep ourselves awake and in a good mood.

Published! The color of LEDs we put outside matters for the birds.

Not all lighting technologies are the same. Since the invention of the first electric light, the development of new technologies continually alters the spectral composition of ALAN emitted into the environment.

In just the past decade light-emitting diodes (LEDs) have replaced older lighting technologies as a cheaper and more energy efficient alternative and are commercially available in a variety of spectra. Variation in spectral composition affects how organisms perceive night-light, and determines biological responses - for example, many organisms are particularly sensitive to the short-wavelength light produced by the most common “white” LEDs (e.g. 5000K). However, few studies have compared broad spectrum LED lights that are commercially available and widely used.

Check out my first first-authored paper! Artificial light at night disrupts nocturnal rest and increases glucocorticoids in zebra finches.
